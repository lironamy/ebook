Chapter 1

The only reason that Dr. Jennifer Kale had ever subscribed to The Lancaster Farmer was because her cousin had taken first place in a pumpkin growing contest sometime in the late ‘80s. By 1997, she was still subscribed, but had no good excuse.

When Agatha ran out of things to make fun of her for, which wasn’t very often, she always had The Lancaster Farmer subscription to fall back on. True that Jen was the PI and Agatha naught but a lowly postdoc, but that wouldn’t stop Agatha from asking her if they taught her to pipette like that on the farm when she dribbled something out of a test tube.

The summer of 1997 in Philadelphia was the hottest that Agatha could ever remember enduring. People broke the caps off of fire hydrants and let their children free in the spray. It was the kind of summer that made one vaguely grateful to be a researcher toiling away in the cool basement of a university lab.

On Sundays, she and Jen had taken to getting an iced coffee and sitting in Rittenhouse Park to argue for a while, as if they didn’t see enough of each other already. Agatha had recently broken up with her girlfriend and begrudgingly embraced celibacy, given that she was ill-suited for its foul shadow, intimacy, and she had realized somewhere at the beginning of the summer of ‘97 that Jen was maybe the best she was ever going to get in terms of a relationship.

When Agatha was mean, Jen was mean too. Their conversations could be mistaken for relationship-ending arguments. That was what Agatha liked, and what saved her from wasting away in her apartment all summer like a shut-in.

Jen lived on an enviable Fitler Square block that might have fooled people into thinking they were in a more refined city—Park Slope, perhaps—which Agatha hated. There were certainly no children running through fire hydrants there.

On that end of July Sunday, Agatha made the schlep up the stairs to Jen’s third floor walk-up. When she arrived at her door, there was a newspaper laid out on her step. A smile tugged at Agatha’s mouth. She bent over and picked it up, studying the headline.

*

The front page of that Sunday’s issue of The Lancaster Farmer was the first time Agatha Harkness ever saw Rio Vidal’s smiling face, and the first place she ever saw Michael.

*

The telling of any good story necessitates a beginning, a middle, and an end. The beginning of this one could have been Jen’s cousin planting that pumpkin seed, or it could have been the many cells that made up Michael’s perfect body splitting akimbo. Or, perhaps, the beginning could have been just there; in the hallway of Jen’s apartment building, Agatha holding the copy of the newspaper.

The photo of Rio and Michael, the banner headline. Miracle at Area Farm.

“Holy shit,” Agatha whispered. And just like that, everything changed.

*

“I just spoke to somebody at the farm,” Jen said, emerging from her bedroom. Agatha was waiting on the couch, a cup of coffee in her hand, “And I have good news and bad news.”

Agatha said, “Good news first.”

“The only place this has been published is The Lancaster Farmer. The calf was born alive. The calf is still alive. Nobody else has contacted them about her yet.”

Agatha’s pupils turned to dollar bills as she thought about the pharma money that was about to roll in from this. The notoriety in their field. They would write papers. They would write several papers, “Alive means we can take samples,” She said in a rush, “And run tests.”

“Yes, but,” Jen paused — the bad news, Agatha reckoned, “They are not keen on giving her up.”

A long silence, “Did you offer them money?”

“Of course I offered them money.”

“How much money?”

“Agatha,” Jen said, “I don’t think the money is the issue.”

*

And so it was that Agatha Harkness found herself spending her Sunday writing a 25 page report on all of the good a two-headed calf could do for society (“Make it bleed,” Jen said, “This woman strikes me as the sensitive type.”). She had no home computer and so wrote half of it by hand in a legal pad at the kitchen table of her South Philly apartment, the window open, the box fan flapping the pages.

When it was finished, and typed up on one of the university’s computers, and printed at great personal expense, Agatha married the pages with a binder clip and put them in the boot of her car.

On Monday, she left for Lancaster.

*

The telling of every good story necessitates a beginning. Perhaps many beginnings. Consider this one: Agatha getting into her cherry red Toyota and pointing it west. Pressing on the gas pedal. Driving forward.

*

Agatha stopped at a fruit stand somewhere off of Highway 222 and muddied her sneakers looking for the ripest strawberries on the table. It had been colder in Philadelphia that morning and she’d worn a sweater, a red crewneck that had Temple stitched on the front, but standing there in the dead central Pennsylvania heat, she felt only overhot and aware that she’d announced herself as an interloper.

She dined on the strawberries sitting on the hood of her car, a brand new (when she got it) 1988 Toyota that she’d bought as a gift for herself when she’d been accepted into her PhD program. A little girl in a bonnet and thick rimmed glasses peered at her from behind a line of dresses strung up with clothespins. There was no wind. They did not move.

Agatha rode on flicking strawberry tops out her car window and occasionally referring to her Mapquest print out. She tried to keep her eyes on the road though her only companions were the occasional horse and buggy. It would be just her luck to become an accidental Amish family annihilator.

By the time that she turned onto the road marked Pickett Ln Pvt Dr, it was going on four in the afternoon. Agatha was too hot in her sweater but had not the foresight to wear an undershirt, and the strawberries, though delicious, had only satiated her for perhaps an hour.

When she steered her Toyota into the long gravel drive in front of the blue farm house, she did so hungry and uncomfortable. Agatha sighed, killed the ignition, and slumped back into her seat for a moment.

In the empty seat beside her, Agatha lifted the Mapquest directions to glance at the copy of The Lancaster Farmer underneath, where Rio Vidal smiled up at her from black and white print. Miracle at Area Farm. It was front page news.

There was a knock at the driver’s side window and Agatha startled. When she turned, the woman from the newspaper was standing in front of her in full color. Agatha cranked down her window.

“Howdy,” Said the woman, apparently unashamed of sounding like a backwoods parody of herself. Agatha disliked gauche people, and so hated her immediately. The woman placed her forearm on the top of the car and leaned in to further disturb Agatha.

“Hi,” Agatha replied, “You must be Rio.”

“And you must be here about Michael,” Rio’s face was round and her features were dark. Her skin had taken on the color of somebody who spent all day out in the sun and she seemed to be fighting against some sort of innate seriousness of expression. There was a silver stud in her nose and her black hair was pulled back in a ponytail and tucked beneath a ball cap.

Agatha figured, despite the air of pleasantness about the conversation, that the dislike was mutual, “Michael?”

“You’re from the university, aren’t you?” Rio continued, “Jen?”

“Not Jen,” Agatha said.

“Oh, the woman who called. Her name was Jen.”

“Yes, but I’m not Jen,” Agatha pursed her lips, “Can you move so I can get out of my car? I’m sweating my ass off in here.”

Instead of moving away, Rio leaned in closer, until her head was half inside Agatha’s window. Agatha angled her body back and felt her face get hot.

“I think it might save both of us some time if you tell me what you’re here for,” Rio said, “I told Jen that Michael isn’t for sale.”

“Who is Michael?” Agatha asked again, exasperated. The gearshift was digging into her back. Rio was looking at her, and her eyes were dark like little blots of oil.

“You’d probably know if you’d read that article past the headline,” She gestured with her chin to where the newspaper lay half exposed under Agatha’s print out. Agatha shot a hand out to cover it again.

“Jen thought it warranted a face to face discussion.”

One dark eyebrow lifted on Rio’s face, “And she chose you as her emissary?”

“How did you even know I worked for the lab?”

Rio’s gaze shifted again, this time to Agatha’s chest. Agatha was about to say something indignant, even if the attention made her a little hot at the nape of her neck, before she remembered what was stitched right at the front. “Go Owls,” Said Rio.

Agatha said, “I’m getting out of my car now. You can move or I can knock you over, your choice.”

Rio took the second option, making her only a little smarter than Agatha first assumed. When Agatha’s sneakers hit the gravel of the driveway, she stretched upward, then shut the car door.

Standing across from her, Rio wore a big John Deere t-shirt tucked into her pants and blue jeans that looked to be on life support. Somebody had darned holes in the knees with bright red yarn.

Her ball cap, though, was from an Ani DiFranco concert in ‘96. Everybody, Agatha supposed, was entitled to have layers.

“The University has given me authorization,” Agatha began once she’d stretched, crossing her arms and trying to give Rio some of that hard tack look that had intimidated professors and PIs alike. It only seemed to water Rio, like she was a big sunflower, “Has authorized me to pay you very well for—for the—”

“—for Michael?”

“Is that her name?” Asked Agatha. She was still staring at the Ani DiFranco hat, and she was trying very hard to not let it endear her to Rio, who was very annoying, “Michael? You named her Michael?”

“We let some of the kids from the grade school vote on it. It was in the paper you didn’t read.”

“The University has given me authorization to pay you very well for…Michael. You just have to name your terms. I have a checkbook in the car and you will find me a very agreeable negotiator,” Not usually true, but goddamn was Agatha hungry.

“So you can do what with her,” Said Rio, removing her cap. Underneath, her hair was damp with sweat. She pushed it back and held the cap under her armpit, “I mean, what’s she going to do in Philadelphia? Go to the PMA?”

Agatha pursed her lips, “She will contribute invaluable data to scientific research that could help thousands—”

“—after you kill her, right?” Rio swatted a fly from in front of her face and sniffed.

“Before that, we’ll run tests, do labs—she’ll be very comfortable.”

“But you’ll still kill her,” Rio pressed, then added, “Eventually.”

“We use very humane euthanasia methods. PETA couldn’t find something on us if they tried. I’ve got all the paperwork in the car.”

“That won’t be necessary. Answer’s still no.”

“If you would just read the paperwork I brought, it discusses—”

“She’s a living thing,” Rio’s face gave into that connatural earnestness. To Agatha’s surprise, the turn of expression made her lovely, “And I think that she has the right to live as much as you or I do, regardless of the way she was born.”

“Of course she has a right to live,” Agatha said, “And she’s lived much longer than I would have ever expected she would.”

“But you’re here to take her away and kill her?”

“That is a very uncharitable interpretation of what we’re trying to do. And inaccurate.”

“She can’t make decisions for herself. If she dies, I think it should be on her terms. Not locked up in some lab.”

Agatha felt her stomach tighten with frustration and hunger. Her skin itched where she was damp around the collar of her sweater, “She just had to be born on womyn’s land, didn’t she? Couldn’t have been on a, a,” Agatha scoffed, threw up her hands, “Monsanto farm or something. I wouldn’t even have to pay them for her, she’d already be dead. I’d just have to dig through their trash.”

Rio’s eyes narrowed and finally she wore an expression of contempt, “All you people ever want to do is kill every strange thing in the world.”

All at once, the hunger and heat boiled over in Agatha. She’d always been good at feeling angry, ever since she was a little girl. She tried to forget the way Rio’s face had morphed, and the way she’d felt when her eyes lingered on her chest, and focus on the anger, “You people? You don’t know the first damn thing about me. You don’t even know my name,” Rio’s eyebrows raised a little when Agatha said this, as if she hadn’t yet considered that Agatha had a name, “You don’t know anything. I drove three hours from Philadelphia, I’m hot and I’m starving, and you are being extremely rude.”

Rio’s mouth lifted into a smile. She looked like she’d forgotten all about the scorn she’d just felt, “I’m being rude.”

“The least you could do,” Agatha continued in a huff, walking to her car and wrenching open the trunk and producing the thick stack of papers held together by a binder clip, “Is read the papers I went out of my way to bring over here.”

She stomped back to Rio and pushed the papers into Rio’s arms. Rio took them, even as her mouth said, “I didn’t ask for these papers. I didn’t ask for you to come here at all.”

“I’m here now,” Agatha said, “And I’m not leaving until you read them.”

“What’s your name?”

Agatha blinked, “My name?”

“Your name, you just said I didn’t even know it,” Rio had the papers clutched to her chest and the cap slipping from underneath her armpit and a bewildered expression hanging on her face. To Agatha’s horror, now that she had recognized Rio as beautiful, she couldn’t unsee it, “And I want to know it now.”

“I’ll leave if you read the papers. I drove 3 hours.”

“I’ll read the papers if you tell me your name.”

“Agatha,” Agatha said, feeling at once vulnerable and disturbed by her own vulnerability. She had divulged her name thousands of times, written it, spoken it, been given it against her will. In grade school children teased her about it because it sounded like a witch’s name, like Agatha lived in a cottage propped up on chicken legs and boiled babies into a stew. That idea no longer bothered her, but Rio smiling did, “My name is Agatha.”

“Well, Agatha,” Rio said, “Here you are.”

“Here I am,” Agatha agreed stiffly, “I can wait in my car while you read those.”

“Do I seem like a person with an abundance of time to sit down and read a bunch of papers?” Rio shifted the papers somewhat in her arms.

“You could just say yes and then we could both go about our days.”

They stared at each other for a moment. Then, Rio turned on her heel and began to walk away. Agatha stood in stunned silence for a moment before going after her.

“Hey,” She called, “We’re not done here.”

“We definitely are,” Rio called back, not turning her head, “Lady, I do not have time to read whatever the hell it is you just gave me.”

“You don’t even want to know?” Agatha stopped, out of breath, hot and upset, “I cared enough about this to come all the way the hell out here just for this, I’m literally chasing you through your farm, and you’re not even a little bit curious why I might be doing it?”

Rio paused, still not turning around.

“Why’s it so important?”

“The papers,” Agatha panted, “It’s all in the fucking papers.”

They lingered in stillness for another moment, and then Rio again began to walk forward. Agatha rolled her eyes. Her head fell back. She groaned.

“Are you serious?” She called.

Rio said, “If you want to see her—the reason you came all the way the hell out here—you can follow me.”

*

Agatha needn’t have worried about her sneakers before. They were ruined by the time she and Rio alighted at the back barn, which had taken them on a journey around the farm house and through a bucolic, wildflower-speckled paddock where half a dozen animals grazed in pasain harmony.

Rio pushed open the door to a small, red and white structure. She had put her hat back on but still had Agatha’s papers tucked beneath her arm. Agatha sneezed, then looked ruefully down at her shoes.

“Jesus christ,” She said, “Is this a farm or a mud and pollen factory?”

“Both,” Rio chirped. She led Agatha into the barn, which smelled like hay and cow shit. They walked down rows of empty corrals until they reached the only occupied stall, “There she is,” She said of Michael, the lone calf, who was shakily trying to get to her legs.

She was vermillion, with white and sable brown spots, and clumsy. God had given her a second head but not the grace with which to carry it. Michael stumbled and then arranged herself on the hay floor like a prawn, all curled in on herself. Four black eyes peered onward from the sides of her heads.

It was as she had been in the picture, two snouts, two half-formed faces. One of her mouths had an underbite and a cleft palate. Eventually, she gave up on keeping her heads aloft and lay them on the hay covered floor with the rest of her.

Agatha wanted to look away, but made herself stare for a moment. She didn’t want to seem weak in front of Rio, who was staring at Michael with a doting expression.

“Isn’t she something?” Rio asked. Finally, blessedly, Agatha allowed herself to avert her eyes.

“She’s something.”

“She’s the same color as you,” Said Rio. Agatha turned to her and squinted.

“What?”

“Her brown spots,” Rio gestured to the calf, then turned to Agatha, and when she turned, she was smiling. Agatha frowned. “Are the same color as your hair.”

“Gee, thanks,” Agatha pushed off the stall gate and went to stand a few paces away, suddenly needing air, “I bet you say that to all the girls.”

“Only the pretty brunette assholes,” Not a big hint taker, Rio moved to stand beside her, “It’s getting late. What exactly was your big plan if I said no?”

“I haven’t heard a real no yet.”

“No, you’ve just chased me around the perimeter of my property like a crazy person.”

“And yet, here we are. Still talking. And it’s not like you’ve thrown those papers into a manure pile.”

“Even if I was to agree to read these, which I’m not saying I have,” Rio said, “It wouldn’t happen until after dinner. Maybe even later.”

“Fuck,” Agatha let her head fall back. Her stomach twisted in empty agony at the mention of dinner and she closed her eyes to keep from getting a look at Rio’s stupid face. She took a breath of the stagnant air and coughed, “Is there any way we can just fast forward through this? You name your price, I agree to it, and I’m on my way to eat a steak dinner at Friendly’s.”

Rio laughed, “That’s your reaction to seeing her? Steak dinner?”

Agatha’s eyes opened and she whipped her head to Rio, irritated, “What do you want me to say? I saw pictures of her in that puff piece in the paper. I study congenital defects, including polycephaly. Not exactly my first rodeo.”

“But have you ever seen it?” Rio nudged, “In real life. In something alive, not in a jar.”

“Never alive,” Agatha admitted.

“And now that you have you still want to,” Rio squinted, “Take her back to a lab and euthanize her?”

“You want to know how that makes me feel?” Agatha bit out, gesturing to Michael’s stall, “It makes me feel sad, okay? I see an animal with a serious congenital abnormality. One of her heads is clearly underdeveloped. I assume from the fact that you don’t have her out in the field that the lethargy and difficulty with standing isn’t because she just had a nice, long, nap,” Rio pursed her lips and looked away, “I’d give her two weeks. Maybe a month. An uncomfortable month. Or, I take her back to Philadelphia, give her a good meal, a snuggle, and an easy death, and she becomes a part of research that will better humanity. Not bad for a calf born in bumfuck, Pennsylvania.”

“What do they call you at your lab,” Rio asked. She was standing with her hands on her hips and a wrinkle in her brow, “‘The Closer’, something like that?”

“I want that cow,” Agatha said, her voice measured, even, “And the only thing standing between her and me right now is you. So name your price.”

“My price?”

“Your price.”

“I don’t have a price.”

“Every person on this floating shitpile of a planet has a price. What’s yours?”

Rio glanced at Michael’s stall, then back to Agatha. With her free arm, she pushed up the brim of her hat so that Agatha had a better view of her face. She was smiling, “I’ll give her to you for a million dollars.”

Electricity in Agatha’s body, bright and hot. She wanted to call it annoyance or starvation, but knew better. Sometimes she liked not getting her way just as much as she liked winning. It was an excuse to use brute force.

“Fuck you,” Agatha bit out, and then turned her head to spit on the floor. Rio’s smile widened.

“I thought you said you were an agreeable negotiator?”

“I’m also an awful liar,” Agatha said.

Rio said, “If you insist on staying why don’t you come in for dinner? Mama Lil always makes too much.”

Agatha said, “Tempting offer, but I think I’ll wait in the car.

*

Ten minutes later, they were together inside the house. It was old but loved enough to be kept good, and fat with history. In the mudroom, Agatha kicked off her ruined New Balances and left them in a pile next to a hodgepodge of other shoes. In the hallway outside the kitchen, she studied the pictures that cluttered the walls and half listened to Rio and—Mama Lil—in the other room.

The women in the photographs were young, braless, pretty, handsome. Some of them had Farrah Fawcett haircuts and some of them buzzcuts. They held chickens in their arms and laughed brightly at the camera while they milked cows.

Agatha rolled her eyes.

“Where is she?”

“She’s through here,” Rio and Mama Lil’s voices became clearer and louder until a curly, grey-haired woman poked her head through the kitchen door and regarded Agatha seriously.

“I thought we told your friend that Michael wasn’t for sale,” The woman, Mama Lil by process of elimination, said. Her eyes narrowed.

“We wanted to make sure you had all the information you needed before you made your decision,” Responded Agatha diplomatically, although she was about two seconds away from ripping one of the photographs from the wall and smashing it to the ground, “I gave Rio some papers.”

Mama Lil’s eyes narrowed further and she pulled her head back, slamming the door shut. There was the sound of muffled, urgent conversation, and then the door opened again and Mama Lil’s unruly head showed itself, “Dinner is in an hour,” She said, “Rio will get you a beer.”

*

Agatha had guessed by the name Mama Lil that this would be a woman who wore earth-toned caftans, and was correct. The kitchen that they sat in was a testament to the journey that had taken her from Lilia Calderu, the name affixed to a framed mugshot from the 60’s hanging in a place of honor next to the wall phone, to Mama Lil. Every inch of the walls were covered with framed bits of ephemera, some of obvious origins — protest signs — some more esoteric — a large banner advertising psychic readings for $1.

The three of them sat in front of a roast chicken at a round birchwood table surrounded by mismatched chairs. Agatha eyed it suspiciously.

“I sort of thought that you guys would be vegetarians,” She said, ”All things considered.“

Rio, who had changed into what were apparently her dinner clothes — blue jeans with no holes and a white t-shirt that read Been there, done that, still plowin’ — put a paper towel into her lap, “We eat meat. Just nothing with soulful eyes.”

“Noted,” Agatha picked at the label on her beer. They sat together in a moment of strained silence, not eating, eyes darting around the table as if at any moment one of them might produce a loaded gun.

“Rio,” Lilia said finally, “This is absurd. What is she doing here?”

“I am very patiently waiting for Farmer Rio to read the papers I brought and that she promised me she’d review,” Agatha eyed Rio askance, feeling a hot annoyance boil over, “Perhaps you could have brought them to the table?”

“I didn’t bring her here,” Rio said testily, “She insisted on staying.”

“I said I’d wait in the car, you invited me to dinner.”

“Well, screw me for having common decency.”

Rio clearly had no idea who she was dealing with. Or maybe she did and this was just what farmers did for fun in want of literally anything else.

“Rio said she’d read my papers,” Agatha said, and took a bite of her chicken.

“I’m not sure I did say that.”

“So you’re going to, what,” Lilia began, “Spend the night here if Rio doesn’t do what you want?”

“I can sleep in my car.”

“Like hell you—” Lil began, and Agatha was about to rise to the occasion when Rio stoppered them both.

“Just calm down a second, Jesus. She should stay here, shouldn’t she? Where else is she going to go, the Motel 6 off the highway? She’ll be killed.”

“I don’t see how that’s our problem.” Said Lilia. Agatha rolled her eyes.

“Call it humanitarian concern,” Rio quipped.

“If you would just read the goddamn papers,” Agatha said, setting her fork down with a clatter onto the tabletop, “Tonight, then I could be out of your hair before desert.”

“Michael isn’t for sale,” Said Lilia, “Which we told your colleague and which Rio told you. I have no idea why Rio is insisting on this—this—whatever this is, but stay one night or stay twenty, the answer is the same.”

“I didn’t insist on anything,” Rio put her hands to her chest and turned to Lilia, “She’s the one who says she’s not leaving until I read them.”

“And I’m not,” Agatha spat, “So read the fucking papers.”

Just then, the front door opened and there was the sound of a pair of boots being kicked of mud on the step. ”Hiya!” A boy’s voice called. There was the tromping of feet in the hallway, and then he appeared at the doorway of the kitchen, all gangly 6 feet of him. He had a dark crop of curly hair and a Green Day t-shirt and could not have been older than nineteen-or-twenty. “What’s for dinner?” He said, and flopped into the chair next to Agatha. As he did so, he finally seemed to notice her presence, “Oh, hi.”

“Do you want some water, Billy?” Rio asked, passing him the chicken. The boy shook his head.

“Rio,” Lilia continued, “Why don’t you go upstairs, read the damn papers, and tell this woman to hit the road.”

“Billy’s here now,” Said Rio, taking the plate back and setting it on the table once the boy had taken his portion, “Can’t we just have a nice dinner and talk about this later?”

Lilia said, “Don’t make William a part of this.”

“Uh,” Said the boy, William or Billy, who had his brow pinched and his eyes observing the goings on at the table, “Sorry, so who is this?”

“Rio, can I talk to you?” Lilia said, wiping her mouth with her napkin and then tossing it to the table, “Privately?”

“Privately” turned out to be out the screen door next to the fridge and into the humid July evening, which pulsed with fireflies and crickets. When they stepped out, a wave of air conditioning blew out and a wave of hot air blew in, and stuck even after the door was shut.

Agatha and William-or-Billy sat in silence for a moment. Then, the boy turned to her and stuck out his hand, “I’m William,” He said, “I’m WWOOFing with Rio and Mama Lil this summer.”

Agatha eyed his hand, then crossed her arms, “Of course you are.”

Outside, Rio and Lilia raised their voices enough that the muffled sound of their arguing was audible in the kitchen.

“Do they argue like this a lot?” Agatha asked. William smiled, the corner of his eyes crinkling, and pulled his hand back. He didn’t look offended.

“They’re both stubborn. And most of the time they’re both right.”

The door swung open and the two women reemerged, not looking very much like they’d struck a truce. They resumed their spots at the table in silence.

“You’re from Temple?” William asked. He was sitting forward in his chair, elbows on the table, like he could hardly contain his curiosity.

Agatha took a pull of her beer, which was going lukewarm, “That’s right.”

“So you must be here to see Michael.”

Lilia’s eyes flashed as if she was getting ready to start again, but Rio put a hand on her forearm, and to Agatha’s surprise, she settled. “Let’s not, okay? Agatha, if you want to stay here tonight, you are welcome to the futon in the farm office.”

“Ritzy. I heard there’s a Motel 6 down the street where I could get murdered. That could also be exciting,” Agatha suppressed a burp. At least now she was full, “Or, you know, you could just read the papers.”

“If you want me to read them, it’ll be on my time. You’re welcome to stay or not to stay,” Rio looked at Agatha from underneath her eyelashes, seeming self satisfied from whatever victory she’d won out in the back yard. Lilia had her eyebrows up around her hairline in a sort of I told you so gesture. William just seemed happy to be there.

“Futon sounds great,” Agatha bit out, and shoved a bite of chicken into her mouth. Her gaze swiveled to Lilia, “So this is yours? This place? It looks like it was some kind of lesbian separatist commune in the 70’s, judging by the,” Agatha took another drink of her beer, “By the pictures on the wall.”

Lil gave her a guarded look, “Good eye. I wouldn’t have pegged you as an expert.”

“I dabble,” Agatha replied, and smiled at the faint expression of surprise, or perhaps respect, that passed over her opponent’s face. “A little light on lesbians these days, isn’t it?”

When she turned her eyes to Rio, the other woman’s cheeks had a faint dusting of pink. She was picking around at her chicken with her fork but not making any obvious progress toward eating it. Some of her earlier bravado had sloughed off.

Agatha almost did a double take. Well, she’d be goddamned.

“Times have changed,” Said Lil, “We do what we can.”

“It’s nice,” Agatha mopped up the last of the chicken juice on her plate with a piece of bread and chewed, “Places like this. I think it’s nice that they exist. Great chicken, by the way.”

In the aftermath of her words, the table sat in a stunned sort of silence, as if unable to cope with the specter of pleasant conversation. Rio cleared her throat and said, “After dinner we usually smoke a joint in the living room and play some records. You’re welcome to join us.”

“Oh jeeze,” Agatha said, screwing her face up, “Fun as that sounds, I think I’m going to have to pass.”

*

Rio showed her to her room shortly after, which was a repurposed study tucked off the back of the house. The space was cramped and the aforementioned futon was nearly touching a scuffed-up oak desk with an ancient computer sitting atop it and a desk phone beside. When Rio reached down to pull the futon into a bed, the two did touch.

Agatha had turned on the desk lamp and the room was dimly lit in amber. The insulation must have been shit because the sound of cicadas screaming outside was quite clear.

“She’s right, you know,” Rio said, fussing with a stack of throw blankets on the futon. She had her back to Agatha, half bent over.

“Hm?” Agatha toyed with the pull chain of the lamp.

“Mama Lil is right, the answer, no matter what,” Rio straightened and turned to her. She crossed her arms, looked consternated, like she couldn’t quite figure how they’d ended up there, in that room. Agatha couldn’t quite figure it either, “Is no.”
