“We thought we would bury her in it. We don’t customarily bury the animals, but it seemed like a nice thing to do, given—” Rio paused and her mouth tightened, “—but I was talking to Lil, and we agreed that it would be alright if you wanted to take her. You wouldn’t have to give us any money or anything, not that you would, because she’s dead. But if her body would do anything for you, you can have it. We’ve got a big cooler out in the garage that we never use, and…”

Rio trailed off, eyes searching Agatha’s face. Agatha kept that hand on her chest. She realized that it was over Rio’s heart only when she felt the pump of the muscle speed up, “It wouldn’t be any use to us. You should bury her here. That sounds nice. That sounds like a nice idea.”

The hot press of Rio’s hand covering her own, “I’m sorry I left this morning,” she said, “I swear I meant to come back before you woke up.”

“I barely noticed,” Agatha lied. Rio’s hand squeezed. She smiled. She dipped her head in.

“Rio, I—oh,” Lilia’s figure appeared in the doorway of the barn and then half retreated as if unsure, “Alice said you’d be in here. Hi, Agatha.”

“Hi there,” Agatha reclaimed her hand and folded her arms. Rio shoved her hands into the pockets of her shorts. Lilia hovered.

“William is outside,” she said finally, after a long, pregnant pause, “He wants to know if he’s digging a grave.”

*

And so William did dig a grave, under Agatha’s watchful tutelage. “I think the industry standard is six feet,” she pointed out when he claimed to be finished at two or three, and when he groaned and rolled his eyes she followed up with, “I’m just telling you what I think it is.”

It was in a nice spot, on a hill from which you could see the cornfield and the paddock and the barn. Michael would never be far from her mother. Another thought that Agatha tried to scuttle, but could not.

In the end, her funeral was attended by six people. It was the hottest day of the year. Agatha still wore her rolled shorts and her scissor cropped shirt and Alice and Joseph joined them, Joseph not really understanding but feeling the gravity of the situation nonetheless. He said a prayer.

Agatha wondered if, if not for them, anybody would have spared a thought to Michael, to her passing. Probably not. And yet there she was, being buried under the auspices of some strange custom that did not belong to cows. Being thought of and spoken about.

“Do you want to say something?” Agatha asked Rio once the last of the dirt was piled on. Rio turned her face up to the breeze and seemed to be listening to something.

After a minute, she said, “I’m sorry that she didn’t get to save humanity, or whatever.”

Agatha looked at Rio, and then back down to the grave, “I think she did alright, for a cow.” She replied, and surprised herself with how much she meant it.

*

Rio gave her a towel and a new pair of underwear and Agatha showered in the upstairs bathroom amid their soaps and conditioners and the eucalyptus hanging from the shower head. When she descended the staircase, Rio was there at the bottom, her hand on the bannister, as if waiting for her.

“Hi.” Rio said.

“Hi.” Said Agatha. She stood on the bottom stair, her hair still damp and hanging over one of her shoulders.

“I just wanted to let you know that Lilia is making lunch. Pasta salad, and then stuff to pick on. Lemonade.”

“That sounds nice.”

“So you’ll stay?”

“Of course.”

Rio nodded and tapped a knuckle against the wood of the railing, “Then after, it’ll be about four, so…”

“Lunch is going to take two hours?”

“Got anywhere else to be?”

“I guess not.” Agatha said, and smiled. She put her hand on the bannister next to Rio’s. Their pinkies rested near one another.

“It’ll be about four, in which case, you should just spend the night and leave in the morning.”

“You think so?”

“I do.”

“You’d be the expert,” Agatha paused, “I wanted to talk to you and Lilia about something, anyway. It’s about Michael’s mother,” when Rio’s eyebrow shot up as if in concern, Agatha continued, “It’s not bad. But I told my PI about what you said, about her stillborn babies. It’s not quite the slam dunk Michael was, but it could be a gene, or—well, we could take tests. Samples. Blood, shit, saliva. It wouldn’t pay ten thousand dollars, but we could give you some kind of stipend. I could get you a good deal.”

“And these samples,” Rio said, “How would we get them to you?”

“You could send them, or we could come to you,” Agatha edged her pinkie closer to Rio’s, “Every couple of weeks or so, at least for a little while.”

“Well,” Rio peered into her face, eyes bright, “We’ll have to talk to Lilia about it, but I guess I don’t see why not.”

For a moment they just stared at each other, and Agatha could feel the enormity of the previous day and the morning and the day to come all pressing down on her chest. She almost said something stupid, something honest, but Rio, as she had been doing more and more recently, saved Agatha from herself right at the eleventh hour.

“I was thinking about you earlier,” she admitted, almost shy, “I was looking out on the hills before I let the animals out this morning and they reminded me of how you looked when you were sleeping, on your side.”

Agatha reached out with her pinky and looped it over Rio’s. She smiled.

*

Heaping plates at lunch, an afternoon spent picking cherry tomatoes with the kid, all of them too full and feeling too strange to eat much for dinner. Laying about the living room with the A/C going full blast. Rio losing the argument against another night of the Cranberries.

Lilia offered to read Agatha’s tarot, and Agatha declined. She had a suspicion that Lilia might actually be able to see the future and was too content that evening to care much about what her’s was meant to be.

*

Later that night, much later, when Rio crawled onto the futon, she leaned into Agatha’s ear hot and wet and said lord, look at you, like the same God that had made Agatha and Michael and Rio and the rest of them was watching them with pleasure. For a second, Agatha swore she felt those eyes on her and shuddered.

*

Rio insisted on walking her back out to her car the next morning. She stood while Agatha got into the driver’s seat and then tilted toward the window much the same as she had the first day they’d met.

“So you’ll be back in a couple weeks?”

“I can be. I can also send Jen, if you’d prefer.”

“Depends,” Rio leaned down so that her hat caught against the roof of Agatha’s car and nearly came off her head, “Is she as pretty as you are?”

“She wishes,” Agatha said, and reached out to take the stupid hat off entirely. Rio grinned; there were her dimples, there were her imperfect teeth. A weaker woman would have given everything up right then and kissed her, so Agatha did. After, she put the cap in her driver’s side seat.

“Hey, that’s mine.”

“I took your cigarettes, too,” Agatha turned the key in the ignition and her car roared to life, “See you in a couple weeks.”

“Hey, wait,” Rio smacked the roof of the car with the flat of her hand, “Your number. I don’t have your number.”

“It’s in the papers I gave you.” Agatha said, putting the car into reverse. Rio slow walked with it.

“Where in the papers?”

“Guess you’ll have to read them again.”

“Wh—Agatha!”

Rio kept her hand on the roof of the car until Agatha pulled away, and only then did she straighten, hatless and befuddled, and stand to watch as Agatha drew all the way down the driveway. She watched the whole time until Agatha was out of sight. For all Agatha knew, she might have watched after, too.

Agatha turned on the radio. The DJ had either fallen asleep or died in the middle of playing the Dixie Chicks’ titular album. Wide Open Spaces played for the first time as Agatha cruised down Pickett Ln Pvt Dr, and Agatha rolled her eyes and spat out her window.

It played again somewhere down highway 222 not far from the fruit stand, in the middle of Agatha’s attempt at steering with one hand and pulling free one of Rio’s cigarettes with the other.

This time, Agatha sang along.

“What it holds for her—” Agatha paused and mumbled along as she lit her cigarette, then rejoined the song once she’d taken a drag, “—she knows the higher stakes—”

She rolled the window down, felt the air. Another dog day, just as she’d suspected. This time, Agatha stuck her head out the window and felt the breeze on her face. She tried to enjoy it. This time, this time, this time, miracle of unknowable miracles—it worked.
